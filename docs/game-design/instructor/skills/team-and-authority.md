# Skill: Team, Authority, and Values (Ch. 13-17)

## Dependencies (Ch. 13)
### The Dependency Stack
Game elements depend on each other. Changes cascade through the stack:
- Core gameplay → level design → art → audio → polish
- A change in core gameplay invalidates everything above it

### Cascading Uncertainty
Uncertainty in one layer multiplies through all dependent layers. This is why **core gameplay must be proven first** — it's the foundation everything else depends on.

### The Design Backlog
Unresolved design questions that block other work. Keep it small. Every unresolved question creates cascading uncertainty.

### Core Gameplay
The fundamental interaction loop. Prove this first, before investing in anything that depends on it. If the core doesn't work, nothing built on top of it will save the game.

## Authority (Ch. 14)
### Taylorism
The idea that workers are interchangeable parts executing a manager's plan. **Doesn't work for creative work.** Game design requires distributed intelligence.

### The Distributed Mind
A team is smarter than any individual. But only if information flows freely and people can contribute their unique knowledge.

### Distributed Authority
Give decision-making power to the people closest to the problem. The level designer knows more about level design than the creative director. Trust them.

### Arrogation and Trust
**Arrogation**: Taking authority that wasn't given to you. Sometimes necessary, sometimes destructive. Trust must be earned through demonstrated competence and good judgment.

### Communicating Intent
Instead of dictating solutions, communicate the *intent* behind a decision. "I want the player to feel trapped" is better than "put walls here." Intent lets skilled people find better solutions than you could prescribe.

## Team Motivation (Ch. 15)
- **Extrinsic rewards** (bonuses, promotions) have diminishing returns and can undermine intrinsic motivation
- **Meaningful work**: People are motivated when they believe their work matters and they can see its impact
- **Climate**: The emotional atmosphere of the team. Fear kills creativity; psychological safety enables it.
- **Playtests-driven motivation**: Watching real people enjoy your work is the most powerful motivator
- **The Progress Principle**: The single most important factor in day-to-day motivation is making progress on meaningful work

## Values (Ch. 17)
Four values for game designers:
1. **Openness** — Willingness to consider new ideas, even uncomfortable ones
2. **Candor** — Honest communication, even when it's difficult
3. **Humility** — Accepting that you're probably wrong about most things
4. **Hunger** — The drive to keep pushing, learning, and improving

## Teaching Questions
- "What is your core gameplay? Has it been proven through testing?"
- "What's in your design backlog? What's blocking progress?"
- "Are decisions being made by the people closest to the problem?"
- "Is your team communicating intent, or dictating solutions?"
- "When was the last time your team watched someone play the game?"
