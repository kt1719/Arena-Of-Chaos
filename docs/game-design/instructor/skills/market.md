# Skill: The Market (Ch. 10)

## Tournament Market
Games are **nonrival goods** — one copy can serve infinite customers. This creates a winner-take-all tournament where the best game gets most customers. The others get scraps.

## Matthew Effect
The rich get richer. Popular games have advantages:
- Stronger community, more play partners, more user content
- Developer has money, credibility, leverage
- Consumer bias toward the familiar

## Innovator's Dilemma
Success breeds conservatism. Successful studios:
- Gain shareholders, staff expectations, entrenched habits
- Lose risk-taking ability, creative fire
- Switch to defensive "cash cow milking" mode
- Get disrupted by hungry newcomers with nothing to lose

**This counterbalances the Matthew effect.** Large studios are static targets.

## Market Segments
Groups of players defined by interests, skills, price range, culture, technology, location.
- Larger segments = more potential profit but more competition
- Smaller segments = less competition but less reward
- **System Shock 2 vs Half-Life**: Both masterpieces, 10x sales difference. Half-Life targeted a larger segment.

## Underserved Segments
The best strategy: find segments that are underserved. Massive profits await — but finding them is difficult and risky.
- **The Sims**: Focus testers hated it. Executives hated it. Became the best-selling PC franchise in history by finding a massive underserved segment.
- No good way to measure untapped segments. Requires willingness to bet big.

## Value Curves
A graph comparing games by rating them on different market values they offer.

**Key insights:**
- The important values are the ones **superior to every competitor**. Everything else can be found better elsewhere.
- The greatest victory is in values with **no competition at all** (BioShock's art deco world, Garry's Mod's wacky creativity).
- Failed games have **stump-shaped value curves** — short and wide, mediocre at everything, superior at nothing.

## Value Focus
Every value costs resources. Fewer resources → must focus on fewer values.
- Large team: 1 unique value, 2 superior, several inferior to round out
- Small team: 1 value pushed to the stratosphere, everything else cut
- **"Better to be the best at one thing than mediocre at ten things"**

Successful indie games have tall, narrow value curve spikes:
| Game | Primary Value |
|------|--------------|
| Super Meat Boy | Ultra-high-speed platforming |
| Dwarf Fortress | Hyper-deep emergent narratives |
| Minecraft | Creative world building |
| LIMBO | Brooding atmosphere |

## Confirmation Bias & Expectations
Players experience games through the lens of what they already know about them. Marketing, titles, word of mouth all set expectations that **change the actual experience** via confirmation bias.
- A review saying "satisfyingly challenging" makes players interpret failure as intentional design
- A review saying "stupidly unfair" makes the same failure feel like a bug

**Designers must regard marketing as part of the experience-crafting team.**

## Teaching Questions
- "Draw your game's value curve. Where are you superior to competitors? Where are you inferior?"
- "Is your value curve a tall spike or a flat stump?"
- "What underserved segment are you targeting? How do you know it exists?"
- "What expectations will players have before they start? How are you setting those?"
- "Match your ambition to your resources. What values can you realistically be best at?"
