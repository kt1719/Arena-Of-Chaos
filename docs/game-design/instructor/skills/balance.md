# Skill: Balance (Ch. 6)

## Goals of Balance
Balance isn't just about fairness. Three distinct goals:

1. **Fairness** — No player has a structural advantage. Critical in competitive multiplayer.
2. **Depth** — All options are viable, creating rich decision spaces. The primary goal in most games.
3. **Other** — Thematic consistency, narrative needs, accessibility (sometimes intentional imbalance serves the design).

## Degenerate Strategies
A **degenerate strategy** is a dominant approach that collapses decision-making. If one option is always best, there's no real choice.

Signs of degenerate strategies:
- One unit/weapon/build dominates all others
- Players converge on a single approach regardless of situation
- The "meta" is a single strategy, not a diverse ecosystem
- Skilled players feel forced into boring optimal play

**The fix isn't always buffing/nerfing.** Sometimes the problem is structural — the mechanics don't create enough meaningful interactions between strategies.

## The Viable Strategy-Counting Fallacy
"We have 20 characters, so there are 20 viable strategies!" **Wrong.**

The number of *options* doesn't equal the number of *viable strategies*. If 15 of those characters are functionally identical or clearly inferior, you have 5 strategies at best. Count viable strategies by observing what skilled players actually do, not by counting menu items.

## Balance and Skill
A game can be balanced at one skill level and broken at another:
- Beginners may find a character overpowered that experts easily counter
- Expert-level degenerate strategies may be invisible to casual players
- **Who to balance for** is a critical design decision

## Who to Balance For
- **Competitive games**: Balance for the highest skill level. Experts will find and exploit any imbalance.
- **Casual games**: Balance for the median player. Expert-level imbalances don't matter if experts aren't the audience.
- **Mixed audiences**: Consider separate balance for different modes/brackets.

## Whether to Balance
Not everything should be balanced. Intentional imbalance can serve design goals:
- Asymmetric multiplayer (Left 4 Dead: 4 survivors vs. special infected)
- Power fantasy (the player *should* feel overpowered sometimes)
- Narrative beats (a boss *should* be harder than regular enemies)
- Teaching moments (an easy section after a hard one provides relief)

## Teaching Questions
- "What is the dominant strategy in your game right now? Is that intentional?"
- "How many *actually viable* strategies exist at high skill levels?"
- "Who are you balancing for? Beginners, experts, or both?"
- "Is there any intentional imbalance that serves your design goals?"
- "If a skilled player wanted to win at all costs, what would they do? Is that fun?"
