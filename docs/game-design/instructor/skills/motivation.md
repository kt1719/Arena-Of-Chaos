# Skill: Motivation and Fulfillment (Ch. 8)

## Dopamine and Rewards
Dopamine doesn't create pleasure — it creates **wanting**. The anticipation of reward, not the reward itself, drives behavior. This is why the moment before opening a loot box is more exciting than the contents.

## Reinforcement Schedules
The pattern by which rewards are delivered dramatically affects motivation:

### Fixed Ratio
Reward after every N actions (e.g., every 10 kills = reward). Predictable. Creates steady but unexciting motivation. Players optimize for speed.

### Variable Ratio
Reward after a random number of actions (e.g., random loot drops). **Most addictive schedule.** Creates constant anticipation because the next action *might* be the one. Slot machines, loot boxes, random drops all use this.

### Superimposed Schedules
Multiple reward schedules layered on top of each other. RPGs do this brilliantly: XP per kill (fixed ratio) + random loot (variable ratio) + quest completion (fixed interval) + level-up (fixed ratio on XP). The layering creates a constant stream of rewards at different timescales.

### Emergent Schedules
Reward patterns that arise from gameplay rather than being designed. A player who discovers a new strategy is rewarded by emergence, not by a designed schedule. These feel the most natural and satisfying.

## Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic Motivation
- **Intrinsic**: Playing because the activity itself is rewarding (curiosity, mastery, creativity)
- **Extrinsic**: Playing for external rewards (achievements, unlocks, leaderboard position)

**Critical warning**: Extrinsic rewards can *destroy* intrinsic motivation. If you pay someone to do something they already enjoy, they may stop enjoying it when the payment stops. This is the **overjustification effect**.

## Rewards Alignment
Rewards should reinforce the behaviors that create the best experiences:
- If the fun is in exploration, reward exploration
- If the fun is in creative building, reward creativity
- **Misaligned rewards** make players do unfun things for the reward, then feel hollow

## Player's Remorse
When a player realizes they were manipulated by extrinsic rewards into spending time on something they didn't actually enjoy. Common in:
- Grinding games with thin gameplay
- Gacha/loot box systems
- Daily login rewards that create obligation, not enjoyment

**The test**: Would the player still play if all extrinsic rewards were removed? If not, the game may be exploiting rather than entertaining.

## Teaching Questions
- "What reinforcement schedules are operating in your game? Are they layered?"
- "Is the player motivated by the activity itself, or just by the rewards?"
- "If you removed all unlocks and achievements, would the core gameplay still be fun?"
- "Do your rewards align with the behaviors that create the best experiences?"
- "Could a player feel remorse after a long session? Why or why not?"
