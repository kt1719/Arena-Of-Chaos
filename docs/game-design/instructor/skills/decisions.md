# Skill: Decisions (Ch. 5)

## Feeling the Future
Players make decisions by **prefeeling** — imagining outcomes and evaluating the emotional response. Good decisions require players to be able to feel the future consequences of their choices.

## Predictability Spectrum
- **Too predictable** → No real decision (outcome is obvious, choice is meaningless)
- **Too unpredictable** → No real decision (outcome is random, choice is meaningless)
- **Sweet spot** → Player can partially predict outcomes but isn't certain. This is where interesting decisions live.

## Information Balance
The amount of information available to the player determines decision quality:

- **Information Starvation**: Too little info → decisions feel random, player feels helpless
- **Information Glut**: Too much info → decisions are obvious, no tension or discovery
- **Balance**: Enough info to make informed choices, not enough to make them trivially

### Ways to Hide Information
- Fog of war, hidden stats, unrevealed map areas
- Randomness (dice, card draws, procedural generation)
- Other players' hidden intentions
- Complex systems too intricate to fully predict
- Time pressure preventing full analysis

### Poker Case Study
Poker's brilliance is in its information balance: you know your cards, the community cards, and betting patterns. You don't know opponents' cards. This creates a rich decision space from simple mechanics.

## Decision Scope
How much of the game state a single decision affects:
- **Micro decisions**: Moment-to-moment (dodge left or right)
- **Macro decisions**: Long-term strategic (which tech tree to pursue)
- Best games layer both, with micro decisions feeding into macro outcomes

## Flow and Decisions
- **Flow gaps**: Too few decisions per unit time → boredom, mind wanders
- **Overflow**: Too many decisions per unit time → anxiety, paralysis
- **Turn-based pacing**: Removes time pressure, allowing deeper analysis. Good for complex strategy, bad for visceral action.

## Decision Variation
Repeating the same type of decision gets stale. Vary decisions by:
- Changing the information available
- Changing the stakes
- Changing the time pressure
- Introducing new mechanics or contexts
- Shifting between micro and macro scope

## Teaching Questions
- "Can the player feel the future consequences of this choice?"
- "Is there enough information to make an informed decision, but not so much that it's obvious?"
- "How many decisions per minute does your game demand? Is it in the flow zone?"
- "Do your decisions vary in type, scope, and stakes over time?"
- "What's the most interesting decision in your game? Why?"
