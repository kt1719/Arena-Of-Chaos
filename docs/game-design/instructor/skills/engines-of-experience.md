# Skill: Engines of Experience (Ch. 1)

## The Core Chain
```
Mechanics → Events → Emotions → Experiences
```
Designers don't author events. They design mechanics that *generate* events during play. This indirection is the fundamental difference between games and other media.

## Emotional Triggers
Events create emotion by shifting **human values** — things important to people that can change state:
- [life/death], [victory/defeat], [wealth/poverty], [knowledge/ignorance]
- [together/alone], [love/hatred], [freedom/slavery], [danger/safety]
- [high status/low status], [skilled/unskilled], [leader/follower]

**The more important the value and the greater the shift, the greater the emotion.**

Emotions also arise in *anticipation* of change, not just in response to it. A reveal of information is emotionally equivalent to a change.

## The 12 Emotional Triggers
1. **Learning** — Epiphany, insight, mastering nonobvious lessons. Deeper = more pleasure.
2. **Character Arcs** — Empathy with characters in conflict. Can be authored or emergent.
3. **Challenge** — Struggle, focus, triumph, wanting to try again.
4. **Social Interaction** — Defeating strangers, saving friends, showing off, building trust.
5. **Acquisition** — The rush of getting wealth, items, power (even fake ones).
6. **Music** — Continuous flow of feeling, usually below conscious awareness.
7. **Spectacle** — Quick emotional rise from big events. Shallow and unsustainable alone.
8. **Beauty** — Pleasure from perception itself. Can clash with ugly themes.
9. **Environment** — Innate responses to landscapes, weather, seasons. Savannas feel safe; deserts feel hostile.
10. **Technology** — Novelty of new tech. Fades fast; must unlock new *design* possibilities to last.
11. **Primal Threats** — Gore, spiders, disease. Cheapened by overuse.
12. **Sexual Signals** — Attention-grabbing but can harm atmosphere and alienate audiences.

## The Fiction Layer
Mechanics gain a second layer of emotional meaning when wrapped in fiction. The fiction doesn't replace mechanics — it adds to them. The **immersive fallacy** is believing fiction replaces awareness of mechanics.

**Fiction-mechanics conflict** is the #1 danger. Crates exist in every game because they justify good cover mechanics. Violence dominates because physical conflict is easy to represent and understand.

The pinnacle of craft: threading fiction and mechanics into a single system of meaning where each enhances the other.

## Experience Construction Techniques
- **Pure Emotion**: Multiple triggers driving the same feeling (action + music + competition = maximum excitement)
- **Juxtaposition**: Combining incompatible feelings (Gears of War: violence + mourning)
- **Antagonistic Emotions**: Some feelings destroy each other (competition kills social relaxation)
- **Atmosphere**: Spread-out emotional background, not tied to specific events (LIMBO, DEFCON)
- **Emotional Variation**: Vary intensity AND valence over time. Classic pacing curve: hook → rising action → climax → denouement
- **Flow**: Total absorption. Requires challenge balanced against skill. Prerequisite for immersion.
- **Immersion**: Player's experience mirrors character's experience. Created by: Flow (strips away real world) + Arousal (from mechanics) + Fiction (labels the arousal). This is the **two-factor theory of immersion**.

## Teaching Questions
- "What human values shift when this event occurs? How much do they shift?"
- "Which emotional triggers is your design relying on? Are you over-relying on any one?"
- "Does your fiction enhance or fight your mechanics?"
- "What does the *worst* experience your game generates look like?" (antidote to fallacy of vision)
- "If you stripped all fiction away, would the mechanics alone create any emotion?"
