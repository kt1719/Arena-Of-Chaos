# Skill: Knowledge Creation (Ch. 12)

## Core Insight
A game isn't an object you manufacture — it's a system of knowledge. The hard part isn't implementation; it's **inventing and refining knowledge about the design**.

## The Knowledge Creation Deck
Think of each method as a card. Different cards have different effects and costs. Master the deck.

### 1. Rumination
Thinking about a problem for extended time. Happens automatically during easy tasks (showering, walking, driving). Also works unconsciously — solutions appear days after you stop trying.

**Requirements**: Knowledge (more old ideas = more possible connections) + Relaxation (fear, anger, and intense focus *inhibit* creativity at a neurological level).

**Tip**: Alternate between problems. While working on one, your unconscious ruminates on the other. Edison, Darwin, da Vinci all worked on multiple projects simultaneously.

### 2. Research
- **Targeted**: Answer specific questions (medieval architecture for a Crusades game)
- **Semi-random**: Expand knowledge to feed rumination. Cultivate unusual interests. BioShock only exists because its designers researched art deco and Objectivism.

**Escape the echo chamber**: Every game team knows Star Wars and Terminator 2. Read microbiology, study economics, learn about Nepalese culture. Unique knowledge → unique games.

### 3. Artistic Methods
Drawing, storyboarding, sculpting, writing short stories, creating soundscapes. Art keeps hands working while recording ideas, pulls you into flow, reduces inhibitions, and introduces productive mistakes.

Orson Scott Card's method: draw a map, name everything, let the world emerge from the drawing process.

### 4. Brainstorming
Good for generating ideas in volume. Bad for refining them. Ideas vary wildly in quality.

### 5. Written Analysis
Structured thought with diagrams, references, logical chains. Forces attention to details that verbal discussion misses. Design documents have value — just not as blueprints for the entire game.

### 6. Debate
Finds flaws in ideas. Requires: skilled debaters, diversity of thought, mutual respect, no power imbalances. A boss who punishes disagreement (even with a sigh or eye roll) corrupts the entire process.

### 7. Testing
Playtesting (most important), usability testing, QA testing, focus testing (market research, NOT playtesting).

### 8. Metrics
Automatic data collection from play sessions. Reveals patterns too small for playtesting (55% win rate differences, rare edge cases). Enables fine-tuning that's impossible by feel alone.

**Half-Life example**: Instrumented player position, health, weapons, time, deaths. Graphed results to find boring stretches, difficulty spikes, and pacing issues.

**Halo: Reach example**: Added a "I just saw lag" button. Combined with replay recordings, let designers see exactly what players experienced during perceived lag events.

### 9. Invented Methods
Create your own. Every project has unique needs. The best method is the one that answers your specific question most efficiently.

## The Organic Process
No fixed algorithm. React continuously to shifting conditions. The right method depends on what you need to learn right now.

## Teaching Questions
- "What knowledge are you missing right now? Which method would best create it?"
- "When was the last time you learned something surprising about your design?"
- "Are you using metrics, or are you balancing by feel alone?"
- "What unusual interests or research could feed your design?"
- "Are your debates productive, or does power imbalance corrupt them?"
