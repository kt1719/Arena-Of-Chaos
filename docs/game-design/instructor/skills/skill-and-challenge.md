# Skill: Skill and Challenge (Ch. 3)

## Key Dimensions
- **Depth**: How much there is to learn. Deep games reveal lessons through layers.
- **Accessibility**: Minimum skill to play meaningfully. Most designers undervalue this because they're too skilled to notice barriers.
- **Skill Range**: The band between skill barrier and skill ceiling. Wide = easy to learn, hard to master.

## Skill Ceiling
The level beyond which performance can't improve. If beyond human ability → **limitlessly deep**.
- High ceiling: Modern Warfare (instant kills possible with perfect aim — unattainable but approachable)
- Medium ceiling: Assassin's Creed II (animation delays cap performance)
- Low ceiling: Tic-tac-toe (trivially solvable)

Players will try their hardest to solve a game, but **hate the designer if they succeed**.

## The Three Reinventions
Games broaden skill range by transforming as player skill increases:

1. **Manual** — Mastering the interface. Aiming, moving pieces, executing combos. All games start here.
2. **Situational** — Knowing *what* to do, not just *how*. Map control, counterstrategies, pattern reading. Where most players live.
3. **Mental** — Concentration, prediction, psychological manipulation. The pokerlike endgame of limitlessly deep competitive games. Why most limitless games are multiplayer — you can learn any system, but never fully understand another mind.

## Elastic Challenges
Pass/fail only serves players barely good enough to pass. **Elastic challenges** permit degrees of success:
- Darts: concentric rings, not just a bull's-eye
- Arcade games: granular scoring (anyone gets some points; experts chase millions)
- Hitman: kill the target (easy) vs. Silent Assassin rating (extremely hard)

## Handling Failure
- Failure must feel like a learning opportunity, not punishment
- Players need to understand *why* they failed
- Quick restart reduces frustration
- **Failure traps**: when failure makes the next attempt harder, creating a death spiral

## Difficulty Modification
- Self-selected difficulty levels
- Dynamic difficulty adjustment (hidden)
- Elastic challenges (built into the design)
- **Emotional life support**: keeping the player engaged even during failure (e.g., spectacular death animations, humor)

## Teaching Questions
- "What is your game's skill ceiling? Can a theoretically perfect player do better than the best human?"
- "Where is the skill barrier? What must a player already know to start playing?"
- "Does your game reinvent itself as skill increases, or does it stay the same?"
- "Are your challenges elastic or pass/fail?"
- "When a player fails, do they understand why? Can they try again quickly?"
