# Skill: Narrative (Ch. 4)

## Four Narrative Tools

### 1. Scripted Story
Predefined events that play the same way each time. Full authorial control but zero player agency. Best for: emotional precision, character arcs, plot twists.

### 2. Soft Scripting
Authored events that respond to player actions. Branching dialogue, reactive NPCs, consequence systems. Balances control with agency.

### 3. World Narrative
Story told through environment, objects, and setting — not through events. Players discover it by exploring. Examples: environmental storytelling in BioShock's Rapture, Dark Souls' item descriptions.

**World Narrative Methods**: Architecture, artifacts, graffiti, audio logs, environmental damage, NPC placement, item placement. The world tells its own story without cutscenes.

### 4. Emergent Story
Narrative that arises from mechanics during play, never authored directly. The most powerful and most difficult narrative tool.

**Key mechanisms for emergent story:**
- **Apophenia**: Humans see meaningful patterns in random data. Players will construct narratives from mechanical events.
- **Labeling**: Naming things (characters, places, items) gives them identity and makes events about them feel like stories.
- **Abstraction**: Less visual detail = more room for imagination. Dwarf Fortress generates richer stories than many AAA games because players fill in the gaps.
- **Recordkeeping**: Systems that track history (kill counts, relationship changes, territory control) create narrative continuity.
- **Sportscaster Systems**: Systems that narrate mechanical events in story terms ("Your warrior has gone berserk after losing his wife").

## Agency Problems
- Players may not do what the story needs them to do
- Players may miss critical story beats because they're busy doing something else
- **Player-character motivation alignment**: The player must want what the character wants. If the character is motivated by revenge but the player just wants loot, the story falls flat.

## The Human Interaction Problem
Multiplayer games struggle with narrative because other players break fiction. A dramatic moment is ruined when another player teabags a corpse.

## Teaching Questions
- "Which narrative tools are you using? Are you relying too heavily on one?"
- "Does your player want what your character wants? What if they don't?"
- "What emergent stories can your mechanics generate? Have you tested for them?"
- "Is your world narrative discoverable, or does the player have to be told everything?"
- "What happens to your story when the player does something unexpected?"
