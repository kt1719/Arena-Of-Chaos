# Skill: Planning and Iteration (Ch. 11)

## The Core Problem
- **Overplanning**: Detailed plans fall apart on contact with reality. Game designs are too uncertain to predict.
- **Underplanning**: No coordination, wasted work, Frankenstein designs, starved stakeholders.
- **Solution**: Iteration — short-range plans, implement, test, repeat.

## Iteration
```
Plan (short-range) → Build → Test → Learn → Repeat
```
- Don't predict deep into the future. Plan only to the end of the current loop.
- Each test checks assumptions against reality.
- Start with broad strokes, narrow over time.
- Early loops: test every hour, make huge changes. Late loops: test every few days, make small adjustments.

## Planning Horizon
How far into the future to plan. Determined by uncertainty:
- **Unoriginal/sequel**: Long horizon (established knowledge). The Sims 2 could be planned; The Sims could not.
- **Original**: Short horizon (everything is uncertain). May be less than a day.
- **Lengthens over time**: Shifting sands at start → established structure at end.
- **Low test cost**: Shorter horizon (why analyze for an hour when you can build and test in 15 minutes?).

## Why We Overplan (5 Biases)
1. **Cultural Habit**: We're taught to plan from childhood. Works for dams, not for games.
2. **Inborn Overconfidence**: A 90% confident estimate is really ~30% confident. Humans are systematically overconfident.
3. **Therapeutic Planning**: Planning to feel better about uncertainty, not to coordinate work.
4. **Group Planning Bias**: Groups reward confident leaders over rationally uncertain ones, even when the uncertain person is more accurate.
5. **Hindsight Bias**: We edit memories to make past events seem more predictable than they were, preventing us from learning.

## Grayboxing
Low-fidelity placeholders that let you test mechanics without investing in art/audio.
- **The screenplay metaphor**: A graybox prototype is the game design equivalent of a screenplay, not a design document. A design document specifies mechanics; a graybox lets you *experience* them.
- **Premature production**: Adding polish before the design is proven. Feels good short-term, cripples iteration long-term.
- **Graybox evaluation skill**: Playing a good graybox doesn't feel like playing a good game. Evaluating grayboxes requires practice. The halo effect makes ugly prototypes seem like bad designs.

## Playtesting
- **Self-testing**: Cheapest. Biased but reveals flow, pacing, balance issues.
- **Over-the-shoulder**: Watch others play. **Stay silent.** Don't help, don't laugh, don't groan.
- **Kleenex testing**: Fresh players used once. Reveals first-time experience.
- **Sample size**: Watch many playtests. Stop when testers start repeating the same experiences.
- **Questioning**: "Tell me the story of what just happened" (memory probe). Never ask leading questions.

## Key Concepts
- **Paradox of Quality**: Temporarily accepting poor work leads to better final quality (more iteration loops).
- **Fallacy of Vision**: A mental movie of gameplay ≠ a viable design. Antidote: envision the *worst* experiences your game generates.
- **Serendipity**: The most valuable discoveries are unplanned. Iteration captures serendipity; deep planning discards it. BioShock's Rapture, The Sims' family focus, Halo's first-person perspective — all discovered, not planned.

## Teaching Questions
- "How long is your current planning horizon? Is it appropriate for your uncertainty level?"
- "When was the last time someone other than you played your game?"
- "What's the most surprising thing that happened in your last playtest?"
- "Are you grayboxing, or are you polishing unproven designs?"
- "What would you do if your core mechanic turned out to be wrong?"
