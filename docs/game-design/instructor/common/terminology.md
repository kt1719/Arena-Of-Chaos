# Core Terminology

Use these terms precisely. When the user uses a vague term, redirect to the correct one.

## Foundational

- **Mechanic**: A rule about how a game works. Mechanics define behavior; they don't define events.
- **Event**: Something that happens during play, generated by mechanics interacting with player choices.
- **Emotional Trigger**: An observation or situation that causes an unconscious emotional response.
- **Human Value**: Something important to people that can shift between states (life/death, victory/defeat, wealth/poverty, knowledge/ignorance, together/alone, etc.). Emotion comes from *shifts* in human values.
- **Fiction Layer**: The representational skin wrapped around mechanics that adds a second layer of emotional meaning. Mario isn't a plumber — he's a collision cylinder. The fiction makes us *feel* like he's a plumber.
- **Experience**: An arc of emotions, thoughts, and decisions inside the player's mind, stretching from beginning to end of play.
- **Engine of Experience**: A game viewed as a machine designed to generate experiences (not to contain them).

## Design Quality

- **Elegance**: Maximum emotional power and variety from minimum comprehension burden and developer effort. Achieved through emergence.
- **Emergence**: When simple mechanics interact to create complex situations. The hallmark of elegant design.
- **Depth**: How much there is to learn about a game. Deep games provide new lessons for a long time.
- **Skill Ceiling**: The level of skill beyond which performance cannot improve. If beyond human ability, the game is *limitlessly deep*.
- **Skill Barrier**: The minimum skill level below which a game is unplayable.
- **Skill Range**: The range of skill levels at which a game presents meaningful challenge. Wide = easy to learn, hard to master.
- **Elastic Challenge**: A challenge that permits different degrees of success/failure, serving a wider skill range than pass/fail.
- **Reinvention**: When a game transforms its nature as player skill increases (manual → situational → mental).

## Design Problems

- **Degenerate Strategy**: A dominant strategy that collapses decision-making. If one option is always best, there's no real choice.
- **Content Restriction**: When a mechanic forces all future content into narrow constraints (e.g., 20-foot jump height requiring all levels to have 21-foot walls).
- **Fiction-Mechanics Conflict**: When fiction and mechanics work against each other instead of reinforcing each other.
- **Flow Break**: Anything that pulls the player out of engaged concentration.
- **Overlapping Roles**: Multiple mechanics/units/tools that serve the same purpose, wasting complexity budget.

## Narrative

- **Scripted Story**: Predefined narrative events that play out the same way each time.
- **World Narrative**: Story told through the environment, objects, and setting rather than through events.
- **Emergent Story**: Narrative that arises from mechanics interacting during play, never authored directly.
- **Apophenia**: The human tendency to see meaningful patterns in random data. Key driver of emergent narrative.
- **Agency**: The player's sense that their choices matter and affect outcomes.

## Market & Process

- **Value Curve**: A graph comparing games by rating them on different kinds of market value they offer.
- **Market Value**: An aspect of a game experience that appeals to some group of people in a specific way.
- **Tournament Market**: A winner-take-all market where the best product gets most customers (nonrival goods).
- **Matthew Effect**: The rich get richer — popular games have advantages in becoming more popular.
- **Innovator's Dilemma**: Successful studios lose their ability to innovate as they become defensive.
- **Planning Horizon**: How far into the future a designer plans. Should be short when uncertainty is high.
- **Graybox**: A low-fidelity placeholder version of a game mechanic, system, or level.
- **Fallacy of Vision**: Confusing a mental movie of an experience with a design for a system that generates experiences.
- **Confirmation Bias**: The tendency to perceive things in ways that confirm preexisting beliefs. Affects playtesting, reviews, and player expectations.
- **Therapeutic Planning**: Planning done to feel better about uncertainty, not to coordinate work.

## Motivation

- **Reinforcement Schedule**: The pattern by which rewards are delivered. Fixed ratio, variable ratio, superimposed, emergent.
- **Intrinsic Motivation**: Doing something because the activity itself is rewarding.
- **Extrinsic Motivation**: Doing something for an external reward. Can undermine intrinsic motivation.
- **Player's Remorse**: When a player realizes they were manipulated by extrinsic rewards into doing something they didn't actually enjoy.

## Experience Construction

- **Flow**: A state of concentration so focused it amounts to total absorption. Requires challenge balanced against skill.
- **Immersion**: When the mental division between player and avatar softens. Created by flow + arousal + fiction labeling (two-factor theory).
- **Juxtaposition**: Combining seemingly incompatible feelings for strange, valuable results.
- **Atmosphere**: Emotions that permeate the whole experience rather than focusing on specific events.
- **Emotional Variation**: Changing the intensity and valence of emotions over time to prevent fatigue.
